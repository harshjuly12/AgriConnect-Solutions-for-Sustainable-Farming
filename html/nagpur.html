Land use

The land use manifests the spatial distribution of land under different uses in agiven area. Eight categories of land use have been delineated in Nagpur district.
1. Dense Forest – They are distributed mainly in Ramtek, Umrer, Bhivapur, Hingna and Narkhed tahsils. Teak is economically significant species in these forests.
2. Sparse Forests – They are distributed in Katol, Umrer, Bhivapur, Mauda andParshivani tehsils of the district.
3. Scrub Lands - These are distributed in Kuhi, Bhivapur, Hingna and Katol tehsils of the district and are associated with extended slopes of the hillocks and ridges.
4. Barren Lands - These are significantly seen in Parseoni, Katol, Hingna, Kuhi andBhivapur tehsils and are mostly the rocky surfaces of the low hills and ridges.
5. Ravine Lands - Usually associated with the streams and river courses, these lands aremore prominently developed along the banks of Kanhan and Wenna rivers and their tributaries.
6. Fallow lands - Mostly associated with denuded mounds and are used for grazing
7. Mines - Manganese and Coal mines are located in the hilly parts of Ramtek and Umrertehsils, respectively.
8. Cultivated Lands - These are extensively spread over the gently sloping plateaus andplains, cultivated for different crops in the district.A comparison with previous land use records reveals that the forest area of Nagpur district has decreased due to excessive human interference and cultivation has extended to marginal lands

Soil Use

The prominent soils occurring in the district are, Black (65%), Red (13%), Loamy (18%) and very small percentage of Sandy and Red lateritic soils. Black soils dominate the area occurring particularly in Wardha river valley, which is most suited for cotton cultivation. Almost all soils are in the range of slightly acidic to slightly alkaline in nature. The major soil texture is clayey encompassing almost 60 % of the area in the district where the clay content varies from 35 to 75 %. The remaining area of the district has soil texture ranging from clay loam to sandy clay loam.
Agro-climatic Zones Nagpur district is mainly divided into 2 Agro-climatic Zones based on soil types, rainfall, growing period and suitability for certain range of crops.
1. Central Vidarbha region This includes Nagpur, Kamthi, Hingna, Saoner, Katol, Narkhed & Kalmeshwar tahsils of Nagpur district. Soils of these tahsils range from medium black, medium heavy to light. Medium black soils are found mainly in Nagpur, Kamthi, Hingna, Saoner & Kalmeshwar tahsils & are suitable for cotton crop. Medium to light soils are best suited for orange cultivation. Main crops grown in this region are Cotton, Jowar, Soybean, Tur, Mung, Urid and Paddy.
II. Eastern Vidarbha region This region includes Ramtek, Parshivni, Mouda, Umred, Bhiwapur & Kuhi tahsils of the district. Soils of this region are heavy to medium, average rainfall of this region is 1200 mm. Main crops grown in this region are Paddy, Jowar, Soybean, Tur, Mung, and Urid

The clayey soils of Nagpur are comparatively rich in organic matter content than others having low clay content, other conditions being the same. Organic matter content in soils of Nagpur is usually less than 0.75 per cent. Water Resources and Management


Well is an important source of irrigation and is found almost everywhere in the district. Irrigating nearly 48 per cent of the net irrigated area, it is a popular source especially where groundwater is plentiful and canals few. Canals irrigate about 26 per cent of the net irrigated area and are mainly concentrated in Ramtek, Parshivani and Umrer tehsils of the district. About 14 per cent of the net irrigated area is irrigated by tanks, mainly concentrated in the uneven and relatively rocky hills and plateau surfaces of Ramtek and Umrer tehsils where rainfall is moderately high and possibilities of water storage is also high along depression/lineament. This percentage is now increased.

Opportunities


MIHAN project Multimodal International Hub of Aircargo at Nagpur (MIHAN)provides an opportunity to develop hi-tech, high value, export quality crops, fishes, floriculture and horticulture crops.
1 Urban Market awareness/Consumer awareness Awareness of high paying urban market of Nagpur is necessary on thepart of farmers. They can take advantage of high demand for fresh and quality fruits,vegetables, flowers, food grains, floral and horticultural products etc.
2 Possibility of area expansion under fruit crops like mango, santra Most of the mango fruits (more than 90%) are coming in the city from states like Andhra Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh etc. Thus considerable income is going outside. There is possibility of area expansion under mango if sufficient irrigation is available during initial growth stages of this crop.41
3 Scope for fodder development- Area expansion There is an urgent need and wide opportunity for comprehensive fodder development programme in view of restrictions on grazing in the forest and at the same time increasing demand for milk and dairy products. Looking to present cropping pattern, very little farm waste is available for cattles. Also area under jowarcrop is decreasing. Very little area is under maize which is preferable fodder crops.
4 Dairy development-Milk and milk products are coming from outside. With rapid urbanization in Nagpur district, there is an increasing demand for milk and dairy products. The present collection of milk from district isonly 12% of the total requirement.
5 Animal husbandry. Consumption of animal products e.g. eggs, beef, mutton etc is rapidly rising throughout the country and the world. This has created increasing demand of animal husbandry products. If international standards and hygienic practices are strictly followed in preparation of meat/beef products, the products have anexport possibility.
6 Organic food production Due to increasing consumer awareness about residual toxicity ofagricultural chemicals like chemical fertilizers/insecticides/fungicides etc. the demand for organically produced food (world over) is on the rise. This can earn considerable foreign exchange for the country with definite effect on farmers‟ income.
7 Sericulture. In order to provide supplementary income and employment to the village people, there is need to promote sericulture, apiculture and fish in a co-coordinated manner

Threats


Natural calamities, long dry spells Natural calamities can occur in the form of inadequate rainfall at the time of sowing and at fruit bearing stages while excessive rainfall leads to stagnated and damaged crop growth. The remedy for these calamities is to have
(1) farm ponds providing protective irrigation
(2) well-planned drainage system to flush out excess water
(3) adequate and timely supply of organic & inorganic pesticides including theequipment's to be used for these purpose
(4) comprehensive and affordable crop insurance systems which should be compulsory and universal.
1 Fluctuating market prices, glut situation It has been observed that the supply of agricultural products is beyond the control of farmers (both when there is bumper crop and there is crop failure).Under such situations, the prices of agricultural products such as food grains,cottonetc. in the market are more volatile than the prices of manufactured goods. The ultimate impact of price fluctuation is borne by the farmers who has no storage facility.
2 No sufficient infrastructure available for storage and marketing Many of rural produce like milk, fish, fruits, vegetables may get good benefit with high value if properly stored and properly marketed. This would required a strong technological support
3 Non-judicious use of natural resources The increase in productivity of farms depends upon toning up of soils with organic manures, providing optimum doses of moisture, etc. However, in this indifference/ignorance, the farmers neglect the most basic practices like 100 composting of farm waste, need-based irrigation etc.Farmers having irrigation facilities tend to use more than proportionate water for irrigation leading to waterlogged conditions in the field, rise of fungal diseases which often lead to reduced yield. This also deteriorates soil health with long term effect.43
4 Farm Credit is mostly used for personal purpose like for daughter's marriage etc. rather than for farming. On the one hand, there is a need for increasing the capacity of farmers to repay loans; and on the other, to provide him loans both for agricultural and family purposes. There is a permanent need for government schemes of Community marriagesso that the loans taken for agricultural purpose will be used for the agricultural purpose.
5 Poor post-harvest quality maintenance at farmer‟s field The farmers have low awareness about quality maintenance, hygienic packaging practices, grading etc. Cold storage facilities are not available in villages.
6 Lack of cooperative marketing and group approach among farmers Lack of co-operative and group approach in production and marketing of agricultural /horticultural/fisheries products is resulting into exploitation of farmers, low returns and consequently low income. Special effort is needed to emphasize co-operative marketing of agricultural produce. This would help to achieve the target of 4% growth rate.
7 Neglect of floriculture and horticulture marketing Although the district has good rainfall and good climate for floriculture and horticulture development, lack of “system and approach” to export marketing of these products is resulting in low development

Machineries


Considering the labour problems in agriculture, farm mechanization is necessary.To a certain extent, farm mechanization helps in reducing the cost of production. Farm machineries such as tractor, power tillers, thresher & harvester and farm equipment such as tractor drawn implements, bullock drawn implements, selfpropelled implements apart from intercultural equipments have their own place in the district‟s agriculture. Presently, apart from tractor drawn and bullock drawn implements, there are 615 tractors, 35 power tillers and 48600 diesel pumps in the district.

Special Programmes and the Ongoing Projects in the District


1 Extension Activities Government of Maharashtra has initiated Mahapic Abhiyan since 2005-06, with a view to bring down the cost of production and to increase the level of average production in each crop. To facilitate this movement, various programmes are being implemented like Transfer of technology from Universities and Research Institutes to farmers field, farmers meets, exhibitions, demonstrations, exposure visits of farmers, Farmers Field School and Village Seed Production Programme. Monthly District Workshop and combined Field visits with scientists of university and extension officials of Agriculture Department are carried out & messages are sent out to farmers regularly. There is good rapport of agriculture & all-line departments with All India Radio & Doordarshan which gives good coverage to extension programmes. Agriculture Extension Schemes:State Agriculture Department and Z.P. operating different schemes to increase productivity through introduction of modern technology in agriculture. Following centrally sponsored schemes are implemented in the Nagpur district.
Integrated Cotton Development Programme: The components of the scheme are IPM, FLD, Pheromone traps, Farmers field school (FFS), training for 53 extension workers, distribution of bio- pesticides, demonstration of organic farming, CICR model and ITK models.
Oilseed Development Programme: The components of this scheme are FLD, mulching, demonstration of ground nuts, bio-pesticide demonstrations, FFS, Farmers training, villageseed production programme. Pulses Development Programme: The components of this scheme are FLD, FFS, bio-pesticides demonstration, HaNPV demonstrations, farmers training, and village seed production programme.
Cereal Development Programme: The components of this scheme are FFS, farmer straining, crop demonstrations, exposure visit, publicity and propaganda. Sugarcane Development Programme: The components of this scheme are planting material production, FFS, exposure visit, green-manuring. Organic Farming Scheme: The components of this scheme are vermicompost unit,biodynamic farmers groups, certification and training. At present the scheme is being implemented in 6 talukas.

Allied crops


Nagpur district is well-known as ORANGE CITY for its large orange growing tract and matchless flavor. The best quality fruits are mostly exported to gulf countries. This generates considerable income.
Apart from this, there is considerable chilli growing tract in the south-eastern part of Nagpur district and it is famous as BHIWAPURI MIRCHI. These crops are fetching better prices.
Almost all types of vegetable crops are grown in the district in the vicinity of 20 km. around the city. Keeping in mind the per capita requirement of vegetables which is 150 gm., and growing nutritional awareness, the demand for vegetables is consistently increasingly. Vegetable crop is a seasonal and short duration crop, vegetable farming is an attractive occupation for small farmers and therefore, the area can be increased under it by cultivating vegetables intensively.
Flowers are in ever-growing demand as the use of flowers is progressively increasing. And keeping the festivals and special functions such as marriages etc. in mind, farmers are inclined to grow flowers during specific season. The area under flowers such as Rose, Marigold and Tuberose is, therefore, increasing. There is great scope for growing flowers in off-season under controlled conditions.
